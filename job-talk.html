What is Index Insurance?

Why Index Insurance?

  • Avoids quantifying individual loss

    • Very difficult to indemnify whether a fisher lost income due to weather or their own luck


  • Fast payouts and cheap administration

    • Money in policyholders accounts in as little as 3 days


  • Payouts immune to fisher choices

    • Fishers choosing to fish less will not lead to fund insolvency

    • Fishers cannot control sea surface temperature

Index Insurance possesses moral hazards

Moral Hazard Definition

Decisions by insured agents that they would not otherwise take if they were uninsured (Wu et al. 2020)

Theory from agriculture

  • Insurance incentivizes farmers to change inputs use depending on the marginal risk effects of the inputs (Horowitz and Lichtenberg 1993; Ramaswami 1993; Babcock and Hennessy 1996; Mahul 2001)

    • Insurance lowers input use of risk decreasing inputs

    • Insurance raises input use of risk increasing inputs

  • Behavior change leads to environmental consequences (Mishra et al. 2005; Claassen et al. 2017)

  • Rainfall insurance in common-pool pastures leads to overstocking and long term degradation of pasture commons (Müller et al. 2011; Bulte and Haagsma 2021)

Evidence from agriculture suggests we should expect behavior change in fisheries

Stochastic Fishery Production

Standard Stochastic Fishery Model

Stock abundance is the primary stochastic variable for fishers in a single period

\[ y=\tilde{B}f(x) \qquad(1)\]

\(\tilde{B}\) stochastic stock abundance

\(f(x)\) concave technology function

\[ y=\hat{B}f(x)+\theta f(x) \qquad(2)\]

\(\hat{B}\) expected stock abundance

\(\theta\) shock to stock abundance with \(\mathbb{E}[\theta]=0\) and known variance \(\sigma^2_\theta\)


\(\theta\) will be called stock risk.

Under standard assumptions, fishers are price takers, incur convex costs, and are risk averse over profits.

Index Insurance will raise risk increasing inputs \(\frac{\partial V(y)}{\partial x}>0\) (Mahul 2001)

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{V(y)}{\partial x} &= \sigma^2_\theta\frac{\partial f(x)}{\partial x}>0 &\text{by concavity of $f(x)$} \end{aligned} \]


Result 1: Index insurance will increase fishing effort if we apply traditional fishery models one-to-one


Intuition for Result 1

Insurance protects against risk. The added risk of additional inputs is protected by insurance encouraging fishers to fish more

However, fishers are exposed to other risks and are quite adaptive to mitigating risk exposure

Flexible Stochastic Production Function

Other shocks may not influence quantity of biomass, but ability of fishers to harvest

\[ y=\hat{B}f(x)+\theta f(x)+\omega h(x) \qquad(3)\]

\(h(x)\) risk effect function

\(\frac{\partial h(x)}{\partial x}\lessgtr0\)

\(\omega\) extraction shock with \(\mathbb{E}[\omega]=0\)

Known variance \(\sigma^2_\omega\)

Fishers can mitigate that risk through the risk effects function \(h(x)\) with “extraction risk” \(\omega\)

  • Risk decreasing input \(\frac{\partial h(x)}{\partial x}<0\)

    • E.g. better gear, more crew, safer boats

  • Risk increasing input \(\frac{\partial h(x)}{\partial x}>0\)

    • E.g. more fishing days, more boats, riskier grounds

Now with two sources of risk and two margins to influence risk, unclear how insurance will change optimal input choice

Proof of IBI behavior change

Fishers derive utility from profits

\[ \pi=\hat{B}f(x)+\theta f(x)+\omega h(x) -c(x) \qquad(4)\]

\(c(x)\) are convex costs and harvest is the numeraire good so price equals 1

Insurance Contract

\[ I(\omega)= \begin{cases} \gamma & \omega<\bar\omega \\ 0 &\omega>\bar\omega \end{cases} \qquad(5)\]

\(\gamma\) constant payout \(\bar\omega\) is the trigger

Actuarially fair premium

\[ \rho=J(\bar\omega)\gamma \qquad(6)\]

\(J(\omega)\) is the cumulative distribution

Set \(\bar\omega=0\), we have good and bad states of profit

Fishers make decisions on the marginal profitability between states when maximizing utility

Lemma 1 Expected marginal profit is higher in bad states for risk decreasing inputs when contracts are built on extraction risk \(\omega\).

\(\frac{\mathbb{E}[\partial \pi|\omega<\bar \omega]}{\partial x}-\frac{\mathbb{E}[\partial \pi|\omega>\bar \omega]}{\partial x}>0\) if \(h_{x}(x)<0\).

Otherwise, risk increasing inputs lead to higher expected marginal profit in the good states.

\(\frac{\mathbb{E}[\partial \pi|\omega<\bar \omega]}{\partial x}-\frac{\mathbb{E}[\partial \pi|\omega>\bar \omega]}{\partial x}<0\) if \(h_{x}(x)>0\)

Lemma 2 Contracts built on \(\theta\) will always lead to higher expected marginal profits in the good state regardless of extraction risk effects

\(\frac{\mathbb{E}[\partial \pi|\theta<\bar \theta]}{\partial x}-\frac{\mathbb{E}[\partial \pi|\theta>\bar \theta]}{\partial x}<0\)

Proof of Lemma 2

Risk averse fishers maximize expected utility

\[ \begin{aligned} U\equiv\max_{x}\mathbb{E}[U]=\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}&\left[\overbrace{ \int^{\bar \omega}_{-\infty}j_{\omega,\theta}(\omega,\theta)u(\pi(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)+(1-J(\bar \omega))\gamma)d\omega}^{\text{Insurance pays out on bad $\omega$}} \right.\\ &\left.+\underbrace{\int^{\infty}_{\bar{\omega}}j_{\omega,\theta}(\omega,\theta) u(\pi(x,\hat{B}, \theta,\omega)-J(\bar \omega)\gamma)d \omega}_{\text{Fisher pays premium in good state}}\right] d\theta \end{aligned} \qquad(7)\]

\(u(\cdot)\) is increasing, concave utility

\(\pi\) fisher profits from \(y\)

\(j_{\omega,\theta}\) joint distribution of independent shocks

\(\gamma\) how much insurance pays out

\(\bar\omega\) trigger value for a contract

\(J(\bar\omega)\gamma\) is the premium

First order conditions

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial U}{\partial x}=&\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}\left[ \int^{\bar \omega}_{-\infty}j(\omega,\theta)u_{x}(\pi(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)+(1-J(\bar \omega))\gamma)\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial x}(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)d\omega\right.\\ &\left.+\int^{\infty}_{\bar{\omega}}j(\omega,\theta) u_{x}(\pi(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)-J(\bar \omega)\gamma)\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial x}(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)d\omega\right] d\theta\\ &=0 \end{aligned} \qquad(8)\]

Use Implicit Value Thereom to analyze how the optimal choice of input changes with insurance

\[ \frac{\partial x^{*}}{\partial \gamma}=-\frac{\frac{\partial U}{\partial x \partial \gamma}}{\frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial x^{2}}} \qquad(9)\]

Focus on numerator because the sufficent conditions of a maximization problem are negative

Analyze change under two types of contracts: First trigger \(\bar\omega\)

Proposition 1 For feasible index insurance contracts specified at trigger \(\bar\omega=0\), optimal fisher harvest will decrease with an increase in \(\gamma\) when \(h_x(x)<0\) and increase when \(h_x(x)>0\).

Proof

Independence of \(\omega\) and \(\theta\) allows us to factor out the joint distribution into the respective marginal distributions shown by \(j_\theta(\theta)\) and \(j_\omega(\omega)\) respectively.

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{U}{\partial x \partial \gamma}=\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}j_{\theta}(\theta)\left[ \int^{\bar\omega}_{-\infty}j_{\omega}(\omega)u''(\pi(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)+(1-J(\bar\omega))\gamma)\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial x}(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)(1-J(\bar\omega))d\omega\right.\\ +\left.\int^{\infty}_{\bar{\omega}}j_{\omega}(\omega) u''(\pi(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)-J(\bar\omega)\gamma)\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial x}(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)(-J(\bar\omega))d\omega\right] d\theta \end{aligned} \qquad(10)\]

Suppose insurance fully covers the loss between states, then utility in the good state and bad state are equal to each other so that we can factor out like terms in Equation 10. For brevity, all like terms including \(\gamma\) are indicated by \(u(\cdot)\).

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{U}{\partial x \partial \gamma}=\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}&j_{\theta}(\theta)J(\bar\omega)(1-J(\bar\omega))u''(\theta,\cdot)\\ &\times\left[ \int^{\bar\omega}_{-\infty}j_{\omega}(\omega)\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial x}(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)d\omega -\int^{\infty}_{\bar{\omega}}j_{\omega}(\omega)\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial x}(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)d\omega\right] d\theta \end{aligned} \qquad(11)\]

The first term outside the brackets is negative by the definition of concave utility, \(u''<0\).

By Lemma 1, the interior of the brackets is positive when \(h_x(x)<0\) and negative when \(h_x(x)>0\).

Therefore, index insurance will decrease input use when \(h_x(x)<0\) and increase input use when \(h_x(x)>0\).

Expected harvest will change in accordance to the change in inputs as \(f(x)\) is increasing.

Contract with \(\bar\theta\)

Proposition 2 For feasible index insurance contracts specified at trigger \(\bar\theta=0\), optimal harvest will always increase with an increase in \(\gamma\).

Proof

Follows exactly as proof of Proposition 1, but with the roles of \(\omega\) and \(\theta\) reversed

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{U}{\partial x \partial \gamma}=\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}&j_{\omega}(\omega)J(\bar\theta)(1-J(\bar\theta))u''(\omega,\cdot)\\ &\times\left[ \int^{\bar\theta}_{-\infty}j_{\theta}(\theta)\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial x}(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)d\theta -\int^{\infty}_{\bar{\theta}}j_{\theta}(\theta)\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial x}(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)d\theta\right] d\omega \end{aligned} \qquad(12)\]

By Lemma 2, the interior of the brackets is negative when \(h_x(x)>0\).

Therefore, index insurance will always increase input use and harvest when contracts are specified on \(\theta\).

Result 2: The design of the contract matters. Contracts on extraction risks could increase or decrease harvest, contracts on stock risks will always increase harvest


Intution for Result 2

  • Insurance protects fishers from risk. Adding inputs increases stock risk by definition. Therefore, insurance will incentivize fishers to increase harvest as insurance protects against the risk of every added input.

  • Some inputs may decrease extraction risk. With insurance protecting risk, those inputs are not needed as much. Lowering input use will always lead to less harvest and thus conservation

  • Insurance contracts can only protect against one margin of risk at a time. Hence why contracts protecting specific risks can only act through the risk effect function associated with that risk.

Mutliple Inputs

Fishers use more than one input

Insight 1: The total effect on harvet depends on the relative change in individual inputs


The total derivative of expected harvest is:

\[ \frac{d \mathbb{E}[y]}{dx}=\hat{B}\frac{\partial f(x_a,x_b)}{\partial x_a}dx_a+\hat{B}\frac{\partial f(x_a,x_b)}{\partial x_b}dx_b \qquad(16)\]

Marginal production is positive, therefore \(\frac{\partial f(x_m)}{\partial x_m}>0\) for either input represented by \(x_m\). When \(dx_a>0\) and \(dx_b>0\), Equation 16 is always positive. The opposite is true when \(dx_a<0\) and \(dx_b<0\).

For inputs with changes in opposite directions, Equation 16 is positive or negative contingent on the relative weight between \(\frac{\partial f(x_a)}{\partial x_a,x_b}dx_a\) and \(\frac{\partial f(x_a,x_b)}{\partial x_b}dx_b\)

Fishers use more than one input

Insight 2: The cross partial of both productivity and risk effect can lead to inputs increasing or decreasing regardless of a given input’s extraction risk effects


Consider a risk decreasing input \(x_a\). If \(x_a\) greatly increases the productivity of risk increasing input \(x_b\), then \(x_a\) could increase with insurance even though it is risk decreasing. \(x_a\) must be added to enhance the productivity of \(x_b\).

Result 3: Insurance increases harvest more than it decreases


Intution for Result 2

  • Risk decreasing inputs have a tradeoff.

    • Insurance reduces the incentivize to protect against risk with risk decreasing inputs.

    • However, less inputs also lead to less harvest and revenue.

    • Fishers choose the marginal utility gain in variance reduction with insurance versus the marginal utility loss in revenue.

  • Risk increasing inputs have a positive feedback loop. Insurance protects against risk, incentivizing more inputs, which always generates more stock risk leading to more demand for insurance.

    • Insurance becomes more expensive at higher \(\gamma\) and fishing technology is concave. Eventually enters equilibrium.

Discussion

Application of index insurance in fisheries needs to acknowledge moral hazards

  • Working assumptions appear to be incorrect

    • Index insurance under a standard fishery model will always increase harvest

    • Fishers internalize risk in complex ways


  • The contract design is important

    • Most propsoed indicies suggest biological triggers (Watson et al. 2023; Hobday et al. 2025)

    • Contracts built on stock risk \(\theta\) will always increase harvest without constraints


  • Harvest increases more likely to occur and have larger absolute increases than decreases

Limitations

  • Risk effects are still nebulous in fisheries

    • Risk decreasing inputs may be a form of self-insurance (Quaas and Baumgärtner 2008), should fishers replace self-insurance protections?


  • How does insurance intersect with management?


  • Biological uncertainity much more complex than multiplicative shocks

    • Sims et al. (2018) embed different variance functions to stock levels

    • Fishers may respond to insurance differently if variance is not limited to increasing stock risk \(\theta\).

Pathways for insurance to change behavior

  1. Moral hazards as shown in this paper will always be present


  1. Quid Pro Quo
    • Fishers may adapt more sustainable behavior in exchange for insurance


  1. Collective Action
    • Tie premiums to the stock health to incentivize sustainability
    • Healthier stocks lead to less premiums

Conclusion

  • Index insurance provides short term relief to fishers

    • All simulations improved utility between 1-3%


  • Without adjusting for moral hazards, insurance could lead to unsustainable fishing pressures


  • If index inusrance is to be used as a policy tool:

    • Identify fisheries that that use risk decreasing inputs

    • Put additional constraints on behavior

Proof of lemma 1

Back to slides

By the first order conditions, there exist optimal values of any individual input \(x\) that must be chosen before the realization of the states of the world. Therefore \(h(x^*)\), \(f(x^*)\), and \(c(x^*)\) are equal across states. Where \(x^*\) denotes the optimal input choice.

Marginal utility in both states of the world is controlled by risk effects and the sign of the random variables. Given \(\theta\) is independent of \(\omega\), the expected value of \(\mathbb{E}[\theta|\omega\lessgtr\bar\omega]=0\). The difference in expected marginal profit across insurance states is defined as:

\[ \begin{aligned} \small \frac{\partial \mathbb{E}[\pi|\omega<\bar\omega]}{\partial x^*}-\frac{\partial \mathbb{E}[\pi|\omega>\bar\omega]}{\partial x^*}=&\mathbb{E}[\omega h_{x^*}(x^*)|\omega<\bar\omega]+\cancel{f_{x^*}(x^*)\hat{B}}+\cancel{\mathbb{E}[\theta f_x(x^*)|\omega <\bar\omega]}-\cancel{c_{x^*}(x^*)} \\ &-\mathbb{E}[\omega h_{x^*}(x^*)|\omega>\bar\omega]+\cancel{f_{x^*}(x^*)\hat{B}}+\cancel{\mathbb{E}[\theta f_x(x^*)|\omega >\bar\omega]}-\cancel{c_{x^*}(x^*)}\\ =&\mathbb{E}[\omega h_{x^*}(x^*)|\omega<\bar\omega]-\mathbb{E}[\omega h_{x^*}(x^*)|\omega>\bar\omega] \end{aligned} \qquad(18)\]

If an input is risk decreasing then \(h_{x}(x)<0\). Then Equation 18 is positive and marginal profit in the bad state is greater than the marginal profit in the good state. Adding more of a risk reducing input reduces the negative impact in the bad state relative to the good state.

\[ \frac{\partial \mathbb{E}[\pi|\omega<\bar\omega]}{\partial x^*}-\frac{\partial \mathbb{E}[\pi|\omega>\bar\omega]}{\partial x^*}=\overbrace{\mathbb{E}[\omega h_{x^*}(x^*)|\omega<\bar\omega]-\mathbb{E}[\omega h_{x^*}(x^*)|\omega>\bar\omega]}^{+} \]

Repeating the same steps for risk increasing inputs \(h_{x}(x)>0\) shows that marginal profit in the bad state is less than marginal profit in the good state.

\[ \frac{\partial \mathbb{E}[\pi|\omega<\bar\omega]}{\partial x^*}-\frac{\partial \mathbb{E}[\pi|\omega>\bar\omega]}{\partial x^*}=\overbrace{\mathbb{E}[\omega h_{x^*}(x^*)|\omega<\bar\omega]-\mathbb{E}[\omega h_{x^*}(x^*)|\omega>\bar\omega]}^{-} \]

Proposition 4.1 In fisheries with two inputs, when \(\theta\) and \(\omega\) are uncorrelated, index insurance will change the optimal use of a specific input in accordance to an input’s own risk effect when the following sufficient condition is true:

\(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a x_b}>0\) when both inputs share the same risk effects, and \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a \partial x_b}<0\) when inputs have opposite risk effects

Otherwise, index insurance will have ambiguous effects on optimal input choice.


Proof

We use the same insurance design from before. Fishers now maximize expected utility by selecting two inputs. Contracts are built on \(\omega\), but all steps follow for \(\theta\).

\[ \begin{aligned} U\equiv\max_{x_a,x_b}\mathbb{E}[U]=\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}&\left[ \int^{\bar \omega}_{-\infty}j(\omega,\theta)u(\pi(X,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)+(1-J(\bar \omega))\gamma)d\omega \right.\\ &\left.+\int^{\infty}_{\bar{\omega}}j(\omega,\theta) u(\pi(X,\hat{B}, \theta,\omega)-J(\bar \omega)\gamma)d\omega\right] d\theta \end{aligned} \qquad(20)\]

Taking the first order conditions yields:

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a}=&\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}\left[ \int^{\bar \omega}_{-\infty}j(\omega,\theta)u_{x_a}(\pi(X,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)+(1-J(\bar \omega))\gamma)\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial x_a}(X,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)d\omega\right.\\ &\left.+\int^{\infty}_{\bar{\omega}}j(\omega,\theta) u_{x_a}(\pi(X,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)-J(\bar \omega)\gamma)\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial x_a}(X,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)d\omega\right] d\theta\\ &=0\\ \frac{\partial U}{\partial x_b}=&\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}\left[ \int^{\bar \omega}_{-\infty}j(\omega,\theta)u_{x_b}(\pi(X,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)+(1-J(\bar \omega))\gamma)\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial x_b}(X,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)d\omega\right.\\ &\left.+\int^{\infty}_{\bar{\omega}}j(\omega,\theta) u_{x_b}(\pi(X,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)-J(\bar \omega)\gamma)\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial x_b}(X,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)d\omega\right] d\theta\\ &=0 \end{aligned} \qquad(21)\]

Assuming the first order condition is satisfied, we can use the implicit function theorem (IFT) to look at the impact of a change in the exogenous insurance contract. Applying IFT yields a system of equations that determine the impact of insurance on each optimal input:

\[ \begin{aligned} &\frac{\partial x_a}{\partial \gamma}=\frac{-1}{Det}\left[\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_b \partial x_b}\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a \partial \gamma}-\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a \partial x_b}\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_b \partial \gamma}\right] \\ &\frac{\partial x_b}{\partial \gamma}=\frac{-1}{Det}\left[\frac{-\partial U}{\partial x_b \partial x_a}\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a \partial \gamma}+\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a \partial x_a}\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_b \partial \gamma}\right] \end{aligned} \qquad(22)\]

Because the determinant (DET) will always be positive by the second-order condition, we can focus on the interior of the brackets. If positive, then insurance will lower use of that specific input and vice versa.

Lemma 1 allows us to sign the partial equations \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a \gamma}\) and \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_b \gamma}\) for any risk effect on either input. Concave utility by definition leads to \(u''<0\). For simplicity, we will only focus on \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a\partial \gamma}\), but all applies equally to \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_b\partial \gamma}\). Insurance payouts equalize profits between different states. If insurance completely covers all loss and \(x_a\) is risk increasing, then \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a\partial \gamma}\) is positive.

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{U}{\partial x_a \partial \gamma}=\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}&\overbrace{j_{\theta}(\theta)J(\bar\theta)(1-J(\bar\theta))u''(\theta,\cdot)}^{-}\\ &\left[ \int^{\bar\omega}_{-\infty}\underbrace{j_{\omega}(\omega)\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial x_a}d\omega -\int^{\infty}_{\bar{\theta}}j_{\omega}(\omega)\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial x_a}d\omega}_{-}\right] d\theta\\ >0 \end{aligned} \qquad(23)\]

Suppose both inputs are risk increasing so \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a\partial \gamma}\) and \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_b\partial \gamma}\) are positive. The only way for Equation 22 to be unambiguously positive is for \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a\partial x_b}\) and \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_b\partial x_a}\) to be positive.

\[ \begin{aligned} &\frac{\partial x_a}{\partial \gamma}=\overbrace{\frac{-1}{Det}}^{-}\left[\overbrace{\overbrace{\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_b \partial x_b}}^{-}\overbrace{\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a \partial \gamma}}^{+}\overbrace{-\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a \partial x_b}}^{-}\overbrace{\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_b \partial \gamma}}^{+}}^{-}\right] >0\\ &\frac{\partial x_b}{\partial \gamma}=\overbrace{\frac{-1}{Det}}^{-}\left[\overbrace{\overbrace{\frac{-\partial U}{\partial x_b \partial x_a}}^{-}\overbrace{\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a \partial \gamma}}^{+}+\overbrace{\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a \partial x_a}}^{-}\overbrace{\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_b \partial \gamma}}^{+}}^{-}\right]>0 \end{aligned} \] Both risk increasing inputs will be raised with index insurance. Repeating the same steps above with risk decreasing inputs shows both inputs unambiguously decrease with index insurance.

Now suppose inputs have mixed risk effects. For simplicity, \(x_a\) will be risk increasing and \(x_b\) will be risk decreasing. The results will hold for the opposite case. By Lemma 1, \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a\partial \gamma}\) is positive, while \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_b\partial \gamma}\) is negative. Equation 22 will be unambiguously positive if \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a\partial x_b}\) and \(\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_b\partial x_a}\) are negative.

\[ \begin{aligned} &\frac{\partial x_a}{\partial \gamma}=\overbrace{\frac{-1}{Det}}^{-}\left[\overbrace{\overbrace{\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_b\partial x_b}}^{-}\overbrace{\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a \partial \gamma}}^{+}\overbrace{-\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a \partial x_b}}^{+}\overbrace{\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_b\partial \gamma}}^{-}}^{-}\right] >0\\ &\frac{\partial x_b}{\partial \gamma}=\overbrace{\frac{-1}{Det}}^{-}\left[\overbrace{\overbrace{\frac{-\partial U}{\partial x_b\partial x_a}}^{+}\overbrace{\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a \partial \gamma}}^{+}+\overbrace{\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_a \partial x_a}}^{-}\overbrace{\frac{\partial U}{\partial x_b\partial \gamma}}^{-}}^{+}\right]<0 \end{aligned} \]

The risk increasing input will be raised with index insurance, while the risk decreasing input will be lowered.

If these conditions do not hold, then it is impossible to determine which additive element outweighs the other, and the insurance effects on optimal input use will be ambiguous regardless of the underlying risk effects of an input.
