  • Fishing is a risky business

  • Storms, marine heatwaves, and other environmental variables directly impact fisher livelihoods

  • Fisher choices and management take steps to eliminate risk

  • Lack of financial tools to hedge against losses

Red Sea Urchin collapse in 2024

  • 95% loss of kelp forest canopy in Mendicino, CA from warm waters and overpredation

  • Divers are going deeper

  • Fishing community is disappearing

Albacore lost 67% of revenue last year

  • Commercial fishers choose between troll or longline fishing

  • Warmer waters expanded Albacore range making it more difficult for fishers to find schools

Fishers are risk averse and take steps to eliminate risk

  • Maneuver around storms or refuse to go out despite losing income (Pfeiffer 2020; Pfeiffer et al. 2022)

  • Choose consistent, known fishing grounds rather than exploring new territory (Holland 2008)

  • Select gear to minimize income variance (Eggert and Tveteras 2004)


These efforts do not completely eliminate income risk

Growing demand for financial tools to reduce income risk

  • Government disaster payouts in the US and Europe have been inefficient

    • Red sea urchin collapsed in 2018. Payouts delivered in 2025, 7 YEARS LATER!

  • Senator Murkowski (R-AK) has called for insurance programs in fisheries akin to US crop insurance

Index Insurance has become the leading candidate financial tool to address fisher income risk

What is Index Insurance?

Why Index Insurance?

  • Fast payouts and cheap administration

    • Money in policyholders accounts in as little as 3 days


  • Immune to fisher choices

    • Fishers choosing to fish less will not lead to fund insolvency

    • Fishers cannot control sea surface temperature


  • Avoids quantifying individual loss

    • Very difficult to indemnify whether a fisher lost income due to weather or their own luck

Index Insurance possesses moral hazards

Moral Hazard Definition

Decisions by insured agents that they would not otherwise take if they were uninsured (Wu et al. 2020)

Theory from agriculture

  • Insurance incentivizes farmers to change inputs use depending on the marginal risk effects of the inputs (Horowitz and Lichtenberg 1993; Ramaswami 1993; Babcock and Hennessy 1996; Mahul 2001)

    • Insurance lowers input use of risk decreasing inputs

    • Insurance raises input use of risk increasing inputs

  • Behavior change leads to environmental consequences (Mishra et al. 2005; Claassen et al. 2017)

  • Rainfall insurance in common-pool pastures leads to overstocking and long term degradation of pasture commons (Müller et al. 2011; Bulte and Haagsma 2021)

Evidence from agriculture suggests we should expect behavior change in fisheries

Results Summary

Result 1: Index insurance will always increase harvest with standard fishing models


Result 2: The design of the contract matters. Contracts on extraction risks could increase or decrease, contracts on stock risks will always increase


Result 3: Incentives that increase harvest lead to larger absolute changes than decreases in harvest

- Norwegian fisheries would increase harvest by 20% as oppossed to 2% decreases

Stochastic Fishery Production

Standard Stochastic Fishery Model

Stock abundance is the primary stochastic variable for fishers in a single period

\[ y=\tilde{B}f(x) \qquad(1)\]

\(\tilde{B}\) stochastic stock abundance

\(f(x)\) concave technology function

\[ y=\hat{B}f(x)+\theta f(x) \qquad(2)\]

\(\hat{B}\) expected stock abundance

\(\theta\) shock to stock abundance with \(\mathbb{E}[\theta]=0\) and known variance \(\sigma^2_\theta\)


\(\theta\) will be called stock risk.

Result 1

Under standard assumptions, fishers are price takers, incur convex costs, and are risk averse over profits.

Index Insurance will raise risk increasing inputs \(\frac{\partial V(y)}{\partial x}>0\) (Mahul 2001)

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{V(y)}{\partial x} &= \sigma^2_\theta\frac{\partial f(x)}{\partial x}>0 &\text{by concavity of $f(x)$} \end{aligned} \]


Result 1: Index insurance will increase fishing effort if we apply traditional fishery models one-to-one

Intuition for Result 1

Insurance protects against risk. The added risk of additional inputs is protected by insurance encouraging fishers to fish more

However, fishers are exposed to other risks and are quite adaptive to mitigating risk exposure

Flexible Stochastic Production Function

Other shocks may not influence quantity of biomass, but ability of fishers to harvest

\[ y=\hat{B}f(x)+\theta f(x)+\omega h(x) \qquad(3)\]

\(h(x)\) risk effect function

\(\frac{\partial h(x)}{\partial x}\lessgtr0\)

\(\omega\) extraction shock with \(\mathbb{E}[\omega]=0\)

Fishers can mitigate that risk through the risk effects function \(h(x)\) with “extraction risk” \(\omega\)


Now with two sources of risk and two margins to influence risk, unclear how insurance will change optimal input choice

Proof of IBI behavior change

We solve by solving the FOC of a maximization problem with insurance

\[ \begin{aligned} U\equiv\max_{x}\mathbb{E}[U]=\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}&\left[\overbrace{ \int^{\bar \omega}_{-\infty}j_{\omega,\theta}(\omega,\theta)u(\pi(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)+(1-J(\bar \omega))\gamma)d\omega}^{\text{Insurance pays out on bad $\omega$}} \right.\\ &\left.+\underbrace{\int^{\infty}_{\bar{\omega}}j_{\omega,\theta}(\omega,\theta) u(\pi(x,\hat{B}, \theta,\omega)-J(\bar \omega)\gamma)d \omega}_{\text{Fisher pays premium in good state}}\right] d\theta \end{aligned} \qquad(4)\]

\(u(\cdot)\) is concave utility

\(\pi\) fisher profits from \(y\)

\(j_{\omega,\theta}\) joint distribution of independent shocks

\(\gamma\) how much insurance pays out

\(\bar\omega\) trigger value for a contract

\(J(\bar\omega)\gamma\) is the premium

Apply IVT to analyze the direction of change in input use with insurance

\[ \frac{\partial x^{*}}{\partial \gamma}=-\frac{\frac{\partial U}{\partial x \partial \gamma}}{\frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial x^{2}}} \qquad(5)\]

Focus on numerator because of the sufficent conditions of a maximization problem:

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial U}{\partial x \partial \gamma}=\int^{\infty}_{-\infty}\left[ \int^{\bar \omega}_{-\infty}j_{\omega,\theta}(\omega,\theta)u''(\pi(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)+(1-J(\bar \omega))\gamma)\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial x}(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)(1-J(\bar \omega))d\omega\right.\\ +\left.\int^{\infty}_{\bar{\omega}}j_{\omega,\theta}(\omega,\theta) u''(\pi(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)-J(\bar \omega)\gamma)\frac{\partial \pi}{\partial x}(x,\hat{B},\theta,\omega)(-J(\bar \omega))d\omega\right] d\theta \end{aligned} \qquad(6)\]

Result 2: Contracts specified on extraction shocks, \(\omega\), could increase or decrease fisher harvest contingent on the risk effects (\(\frac{\partial h(x)}{\partial x}\)) of the input

Result 3: Contracts specified on biological stock shocks, \(\theta\), will always increase fisher harvest


Intution: Insurance contracts protect risk along their specific margins. Fishers use that channel to reduce or increase inputs. Because of the implicit risk increasing nature of \(f(x)\), \(\theta\) contracts lead to harvest increases.
